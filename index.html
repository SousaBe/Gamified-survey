The Future on Your Plate: Exploring Citizens' Visions for Maize Consumption in 2050

This questionnaire is part of a scientific research project exploring possible futures of food consumption, with a special focus on how maize might be consumed in the year 2050.

Your responses will directly contribute to a citizen science initiative, helping researchers build participatory foresight scenarios about the future of maize. Maize in 2050

Throughout your journey in 2050, maize appeared in many forms, from nutritious breakfasts to cultural dinners. Sometimes traditional, sometimes innovative.

But what does it all mean to you? Let‚Äôs pause for a moment and reflect on how you see maize now, and what role it might play in the future of food.

Maize (Zea mays L.), often referred to as corn, is one of the most important cereal crops worldwide, both in terms of volume and versatility. It is the world‚Äôs most widely grown cereal, with annual production exceeding one billion tons, driven by its high yield potential and diverse uses. In Europe, maize represents a significant share of cereal production, and the continent plays a crucial role in the global seed market. With around 30 million seed doses produced annually, Europe is a leader in maize seed production, and France, in particular, stands out. In 2021, it accounted for nearly half of the EU‚Äôs seed output, exporting 60% of its production.

This strategic role in seed production reflects maize‚Äôs broader economic and geopolitical importance. While the United States, China, Brazil, and Argentina continue to dominate overall maize production and trade, shifting demand from low- and middle-income countries is reshaping global supply chains and innovation priorities. As the world faces rising challenges from climate change and population growth, maize will be at the center of efforts to enhance food security, making advances in sustainability, climate resilience, and nutritional quality more urgent than ever.

Moreover, maize is increasingly recognized for its potential in health promotion. Its components have been shown to reduce risks of chronic diseases such as diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and certain cancers. Notably, resistant starch in maize improves gut health and metabolic outcomes, while carotenoids like lutein and zeaxanthin support eye health and may reduce oxidative stress.

By understanding this multifaceted role, from economic engine and climate-resilient crop to health-promoting food, we can better imagine the place maize might occupy in tomorrow‚Äôs food systems.
